**R6 C2**: 1. SPEED = TRUST
(Response Time Bias)

**R6 C3**: Leads responded to within 5 minutes convert 21x more (Convoso). 78% of buyers choose the FIRST company to respond (LeadAngel). Sending proposals within 24hrs increases close rate by 25% (Better Proposals, 100K+ proposals). Home service providers who respond in under 5 minutes often DOUBLE their conversion rate (Improve & Grow).

**R6 C4**: Quote delivered 0-2 hours after form submit. Instant SMS acknowledgment at second 0. No competitor in our market does this — Jordan is ~24hrs, Grout Guy requires an in-home visit, Bathroom Werx requires a phone call.

**R7 C2**: 2. LOSS AVERSION
(Kahneman & Tversky, 1979)

**R7 C3**: People feel losses ~2x more intensely than equivalent gains. The pain of losing $100 is psychologically twice as strong as the pleasure of gaining $100. Loss-framed messages outperform gain-framed messages for action-driving.

**R7 C4**: Frame around what they LOSE by waiting: "Every week the mould spreads, the grout crumbles further, and the fix gets more expensive." Show their ugly bathroom photo NEXT TO a beautiful after photo. The gap = the loss they're living with.

**R8 C2**: 3. ENDOWMENT EFFECT
(Thaler, 1980)

**R8 C3**: People value things more once they feel ownership. Sellers demand ~2x what buyers offer for the same item. Even brief possession creates attachment. Showing someone "their" quote creates psychological ownership of the outcome.

**R8 C4**: Show THEIR photos back to them in the quote — "Here's your bathroom." Use "your" language throughout: "your quote," "your date," "your technician." The moment they see their own bathroom in a professional document, they mentally own the transformation.

**R9 C2**: 4. IKEA EFFECT
(Norton, Mochon & Ariely, 2012)

**R9 C3**: People value things 63% more when they helped create them. The effort of submitting photos and details creates investment. Participants who built IKEA furniture paid 63% more than those who inspected pre-built versions.

**R9 C4**: The customer already invested 90 seconds uploading photos, picking options, entering details. By showing their photos + choices in the quote, we validate that effort. Their sunk cost makes walking away psychologically painful.

**R10 C2**: 5. COMMITMENT & CONSISTENCY
(Cialdini, 1984)

**R10 C3**: Once people take a small step, they feel compelled to stay consistent with that action. Small commitments lead to larger ones. "Foot in the door" technique: agreeing to a small request makes you 2-3x more likely to agree to a larger one.

**R10 C4**: The form was the first commitment. The quote validates it. The deposit is tiny (10%) — just $80-$200 on most jobs. "You've already done the hard part — just lock in your date." The deposit feels like a natural next micro-step, not a big decision.

**R11 C2**: 6. ANCHORING
(Tversky & Kahneman, 1974)

**R11 C3**: The first number people see anchors all subsequent judgments. Higher anchors lead to higher acceptable prices. The anchor doesn't even need to be related to the price to influence judgment.

**R11 C4**: Anchor against REPLACEMENT cost: "A full bathroom renovation costs $15,000-$30,000. Your resurfacing: $1,660." Frame the deposit against daily spend: "Your deposit is less than a weekend coffee run." Never show the deposit first — show the total, THEN the deposit.

**R12 C2**: 7. SOCIAL PROOF
(Cialdini, 1984)

**R12 C3**: 88% of consumers trust reviews as much as personal recommendations. Video testimonials lift conversions by 80%. Before-and-after photos are the #1 most persuasive visual for home services. Specific suburb-matched testimonials outperform generic ones.

**R12 C4**: Include before/after from a SIMILAR job in their area. Show Google rating with star count. Use a testimonial from their suburb or a nearby one. "Here's what we did in [their suburb] last month." Specificity = believability.

**R13 C2**: 8. PAIN OF PAYING
(Prelec & Loewenstein, 1998)

**R13 C3**: Paying activates the same brain regions as physical pain (insula). Splitting payment into smaller amounts reduces pain. Delaying the larger payment until AFTER receiving value reduces friction. "Pay later" options increase conversion 20-30%.

**R13 C4**: Deposit is only 10%. Balance due AFTER the job is done AND they're happy. No mention of "payment" in the CTA — instead "Lock in your date." The deposit is framed as securing availability, not as a cost. Stripe = one tap, minimal friction.

**R14 C2**: 9. PROCESSING FLUENCY
(Reber & Schwarz, 1999)

**R14 C3**: Information that's easy to process is judged as more trustworthy, more true, and more valuable. Simple fonts, clear layouts, and short sentences increase perceived credibility. Proposals under 11 pages win more than those over 13 pages (Proposify data).

**R14 C4**: Quote page is ONE scroll. No jargon. No multi-page PDF. No "terms and conditions" before the price. Price is in a big highlighted box. CTA button is unmissable. Mobile-first design (75%+ of our traffic will be mobile).

**R15 C2**: 10. URGENCY (Real, Not Fake)
(Reactance Theory)

**R15 C3**: Real urgency increases CTA conversion by up to 332% (WiserNotify). BUT fake urgency backfires — consumers actively RESIST marketing they perceive as manipulative. Authentic scarcity works; manufactured scarcity destroys trust.

**R15 C4**: We removed urgency surcharges. But real urgency exists: "Quote valid 7 days" is genuine (sub availability changes). "Mould spreads" is genuine. We NEVER use fake countdown timers or "only 2 spots left!" lies. Authenticity is the brand.
